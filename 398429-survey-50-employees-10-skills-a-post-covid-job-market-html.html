Survey: 50% of employees will need these 10 skills in a post-Covid job market

Posted by Chauncey Koziol on Friday, February 9, 2024

We are gearing up for another year, meaning there will be lots of reviews and new challenges as we try to figure out where our weaknesses lie so as to improve.

Most people tend to focus on the skills they already have but as the coronavirus pandemic has shown us, there is a need to readjust our skills and talent to a digitally-led mindset and orientation.

Technical proficiencies are good to have but you also need to give enough attention to soft skills, which relate to your work style and personality attribute.

Whether you are hoping for a promotion, looking for a new gig or need to get more funding for your business, being able to illustrate soft skill strengths like creative/analytical thinking or problem-solving will make a huge impact.

According to the Future of Jobs report (3rd edition) by the World Economic Forum (WEF), half of all workers in the world will need reskilling by the year 2025.

This is due to the adoption of technology as the double-disruption the pandemic came with and increasing automation takes hold.

Newly emerging in 2020 were skills in stress tolerance, active learning, flexibility and resilience. However, the main skills that will grow in prominence from 2021 onwards are problem-solving and critical thinking.

The report, which maps future jobs, skills and tracks the pace of change and direction of travel reveals that the same disruption can offer the key to creating jobs and help people learn new skills.

The WEF estimates that 85 million jobs may be displaced by 2025 owing to a shift in the division of labour between man and machines. But 97 million jobs may emerge during that shift, which are more adapted to this division of labour.

Among the top in-demand skills the WEF believes will be required as jobs change over the next five years include digital marketing, product marketing and human-computer interaction.

In terms of soft skills, workers will need to be proficient in critical thinking, problem-solving, active learning, resilience, stress tolerance, flexibility, data and Artificial Intelligence.

The WEF report further notes that while an online learning programme could help people gain a new skill set through digital technologies, individuals will also need the funding and time to pursue new opportunities.
